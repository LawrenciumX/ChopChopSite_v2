Chefs & Stories · San Sebastián, Spain

Pintxos &
Passion

· 10 min read · Jean Claude Ndoumbi

By seven in the evening, the Parte Vieja of San Sebastián has the energy of a room that has already decided something good is about to happen. The bars are three deep at the counter. The noise is a wall. And on every zinc surface in sight, an arrangement of small plates sits waiting — bread, anchovy, pepper, a single toothpick — for someone to claim it before they've even ordered a drink.

This is pintxos culture, and to call it Spanish tapas, as outsiders often do, is to miss something the locals consider essential. A tapa is offered. A pintxo — from pinchar, to pierce or skewer — is taken. The food sits on the bar, speared or stacked, and you serve yourself, then settle the bill later by however many toothpicks remain on your plate. It is a system built entirely on trust, and in San Sebastián, trust is taken seriously.

We spent five nights moving bar to bar through the old town with a local chef as our guide, trying to understand how a city of fewer than 190,000 people came to hold more Michelin stars per capita than almost anywhere on earth, and why so much of that ambition still gets funneled into food you eat standing up, off a toothpick, for two euros a piece.

The Geography of a Crawl

Maite Lasa has tended bar in the Parte Vieja for twenty-two years, the last eleven of them at her own place, a narrow room with standing space for maybe thirty people and a counter that holds her entire reputation. "You do not go to one bar for dinner here," she tells us, setting out a tray of grilled mushrooms. "You go to five. Each one does one thing better than anybody else. You would be foolish to make them try to do everything."

This is the logic of the pintxos crawl, or txikiteo — a few bites and a small glass of txakoli at one bar, then on to the next. Maite's place is known for a single dish: a skewer of anchovy, pepper, and olive, so precise in its proportions that regulars order it simply by pointing. She has never changed the recipe. "My mother made it this way. I tried once to add something. My father, before he died, told me not to be an idiot." She laughs. "He was right."

"A pintxo has one chance to be perfect. There is no second bite to fix a mistake — it is two bites, maybe one. Everything has to be correct the first time."

— Maite Lasa, Bar owner, Parte Vieja

What looks like simplicity, up close, reveals itself as discipline. The mushrooms are seared to order, never held. The bread is sourced from a bakery six doors down that has supplied the same handful of bars for three generations. Even the toothpicks — plain wood, nothing decorative — are chosen deliberately, because a flashy presentation would suggest the food needed help. In San Sebastián, that suggestion is an insult.

Where Ambition Goes Small

San Sebastián's fine-dining reputation is well documented — the city has long punched far above its size in Michelin recognition, and chefs from its starred kitchens are frequently credited with shaping the techniques behind modern Basque and Spanish cuisine. What's less understood from outside is how porous the line is between those kitchens and the pintxos bars a few streets away.

One Counter, Two Worlds

Chef Iker Etxeberria runs a small tasting kitchen with a single nightly seating, but two evenings a week he can be found behind the bar at his cousin's pintxos place, plating the same care into a €2 skewer that he'd put into a fourteen-course menu. "People think these are different jobs," he says, slicing cured tuna into translucent strips. "They are not. The discipline is identical. The portion is just smaller."

He credits this culture — high ambition applied to humble formats — with much of the city's culinary reputation. "A young cook here learns to make one perfect bite before anyone lets them near a tasting menu. You cannot hide behind a sauce when the whole dish is two centimeters wide." He shows us a single anchovy fillet, cured in-house over four days, laid across a sliver of toasted bread with nothing else. "This took four days and it will be eaten in four seconds. That ratio does not bother me. It is the whole point."

"In a big kitchen, a mistake can hide in twelve other components. At the bar, there is nowhere to hide. I think that's why this city cooks the way it does — everywhere, all the time, like someone is watching. Someone always is."

— Chef Iker Etxeberria, San Sebastián

The Rules Nobody Writes Down

There is etiquette here, unspoken but absolute. You do not linger at a bar once you've finished eating, since the space belongs to the next round of hungry arrivals. You keep your toothpicks on your plate, not the bar top, so the bartender can tally honestly. You order a small glass, not a large one, because the crawl rewards pacing over volume. We broke at least one of these rules on our first night and were corrected, gently but unmistakably, by a stranger standing beside us.

"Tourists think we are unfriendly when we tell them these things," Maite says. "We are not unfriendly. We are protective. This is not a show we put on. This is just dinner. We want you to enjoy dinner the way we do." She slides a final skewer across the counter — txangurro, spider crab, mounded delicately onto a crisp of bread — and gestures for us to eat it before it loses its temperature. "Now. Not in five minutes. Now."

What Stays With You

On our last night, Maite closed up after midnight and walked with us along the harbor, past bars still spilling noise onto the cobblestones. We asked her what she thought made San Sebastián's food culture different from anywhere else we'd been. She thought about it for a while before answering, which surprised us, since she rarely paused before saying anything else all week.

"Maybe nothing is different," she said finally. "Maybe everywhere loves food this much, secretly. We just stopped pretending not to a long time ago." She shrugged, the way she had shrugged at the end of nearly every story she told us, as if the explanation mattered less than the eating. "Go home. Eat something small. Eat it like it's the only thing you'll get tonight."
