Chefs & Stories · Tokyo, Japan

The Last
Shokunin

· 12 min read · Jean Claude Ndoumbi

Masaru Hoshi has been making sushi for fifty-three years. He arrives at his stall in Tsukiji Outer Market at 3:30 a.m., before the tourists, before the other vendors, before the city has quite decided to wake. He does not find this extraordinary.

The first thing you notice about Masaru is his hands. They move with a kind of settled certainty — not hurried, not slow — the way water finds its level. He has shaped rice perhaps fifteen million times. The count no longer interests him. What interests him is whether today's rice is the same as yesterday's, and if not, why not, and what the weather had to do with it.

"Shokunin" is a Japanese word that resists easy translation. It means craftsperson, but it carries within it the full weight of a life consecrated to a single skill. It implies something monastic — a vow, not just a job. When we visited Masaru over three days in the spring of 2024, we were trying to understand what it looks like to live inside that vow, from the inside.

The Weight of Repetition

He trained under his father, who trained under his father, who learned from a fisherman in Chiba who had no formal training at all — only decades of proximity to good fish. Masaru does not romanticise this lineage. "They were better than me," he says flatly, without false modesty. "I learned from people who were better. That is the only way."

His rice is cooked in a vessel that is older than he is, a hangiri — a flat-bottomed cypress tub — that belonged to his grandfather. He mixes the vinegar by feel. He has never written down a ratio. When we asked him what would happen if the tub broke, he was quiet for a long moment. "I would find another tub," he said finally. "The rice does not care about the container."

"I do not make sushi. I try, every morning, not to make a mistake. After fifty years, I have fewer mistakes. That is all."

— Masaru Hoshi, Tsukiji Outer Market

He serves only at the counter — eight seats, first-come. There is no reservation system. There is no social media presence. There is no sign, only a hand-painted board his wife made in 1987. Most of his customers have been coming for decades. Several have brought their children, who are now bringing their children. Masaru greets each of them by name but does not appear to perform warmth. The warmth is simply present, like the rice steam.

A Morning in the Market

We followed Masaru on his pre-dawn rounds through Tsukiji. He walked slowly, unhurried, stopping at three stalls. At each he touched the fish before he spoke. He pressed his thumb into tuna and felt something we couldn't see. At one stall he shook his head and moved on. "Yesterday's," he said. At another he stopped and talked for twenty minutes with the vendor, a man his age whose own father had sold to his father. No transaction, just conversation. He bought the fish on the way back.

On the Nature of Perfection

At one point, between the market and the opening of the stall, we asked Masaru whether he thought his sushi was the best in Tokyo. He found this question interesting rather than flattering. He thought about it seriously, the way someone considers a mathematical problem. "No," he said. "There are younger chefs with better technique. Better equipment. Ingredients I cannot afford." A pause. "But I have been doing this longer than they have been alive. I know what it is supposed to taste like. That is different from being the best."

There is a concept in Japanese aesthetics — katachi, or form — that describes the point at which technique becomes invisible. The craft disappears into the result. Watching Masaru work, you understand this not as philosophy but as observation. The rice appears in his hand and then it is gone. The fish is placed and then it belongs exactly where it is. There is no visible decision-making. There is only the thing, done.

"A young chef tries to make beautiful sushi. After a long time, you stop trying. The sushi becomes beautiful or it doesn't. You learn to understand why."

— Masaru Hoshi

What Gets Passed On

Masaru has one apprentice, a young man named Kenji who has been with him for four years. Kenji does not make sushi. He cleans, prepares rice, observes. Masaru says this will continue for at least another two years. "He is impatient," Masaru says, not unkindly. "That is fine. The work will cure it."

We asked Masaru what he hoped Kenji would learn from him beyond technique. He seemed to find the distinction puzzling. "Technique is everything," he said. "But technique includes how you treat the fish. How you greet a customer. How you clean the counter at closing. It is all technique." He picked up a piece of tuna from the morning's purchase and held it to the light. "If I teach him to be lazy in one thing, he will be lazy in another. There is no separation."

On our last morning with Masaru, we arrived to find him at the counter alone, an hour before opening, eating a bowl of plain rice. He offered us some. It was, improbably, the best rice any of us had ever tasted — barely seasoned, perfectly cooked, unremarkable by any objective measure. We asked him what he had done to it. He looked at us as though we had asked the wrong question. "Nothing," he said. "I cooked it."
